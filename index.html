How Sterling Goods Grew E-Commerce Revenue 42% in Six Months

Prepared by JLS Research  ·  April 2026  ·  6 min read

Executive Summary

Sterling Goods, a mid-market home-and-kitchen retailer, faced flat year-over-year revenue entering Q3 2025 despite steady traffic growth. Through a coordinated three-pillar program spanning dynamic pricing, retention operations, and paid acquisition efficiency, the company lifted direct-to-consumer revenue 42% over six months while holding contribution margin flat. The program required no additional headcount and paid back within ten weeks.

The Challenge

Sterling entered the planning period with a clear paradox: organic sessions were up 18% year-over-year, but revenue per session had declined 9%. Competitive pressure from two new marketplace entrants had compressed list prices on the company's top three SKUs, and an aging email program was producing diminishing repeat-purchase rates. Leadership set a goal of double-digit revenue growth without increasing discount depth or expanding the paid channel mix.

Strategy 1 — Dynamic Pricing Implementation

The team deployed a rules-based pricing engine across 340 core SKUs, refreshing list prices every four hours against a basket of seven competitor feeds. Rather than racing competitors to the bottom, the engine was tuned to hold Sterling's price within a 2.5% band of the median competitor price, trading a small volume concession on hyper-commoditized items for protected margin on differentiated bundles. Within eight weeks, average selling price rose 4.1% on the top quartile of SKUs while unit velocity held steady.

Strategy 2 — Customer Retention Program

Sterling rebuilt its post-purchase lifecycle around behavioral triggers rather than calendar-driven sends. A consolidated customer data layer fed seven trigger-based flows — replenishment reminders, cross-category nudges, win-back sequences, and a VIP early-access cohort for top-decile buyers. The program increased 90-day repeat-purchase rate from 14% to 23% and, more critically, doubled the contribution of second-order revenue to the trailing quarter.

Strategy 3 — Paid Acquisition Optimization

On the acquisition side, Sterling shifted from last-click ROAS targets to a contribution-margin-aware bidding model, retiring seventeen underperforming ad groups and reallocating spend toward high-LTV creative variants. A tighter feedback loop between the merchandising team and the paid-media team surfaced inventory-aware bidding opportunities, letting the company lean into categories with surplus stock and pull back on near-stockouts before they hit the landing page.

Headline outcome: 42% lift in direct revenue, 4.1% improvement in average selling price, and a 9-point jump in 90-day repeat-purchase rate — delivered inside a single fiscal half without additional headcount.

Results & Key Takeaways

  • Direct-to-consumer revenue grew 42% over the six-month program window.
  • Average selling price on top-quartile SKUs rose 4.1%, reversing a multi-quarter decline.
  • 90-day repeat-purchase rate climbed from 14% to 23%; second-order revenue share nearly doubled.
  • Paid acquisition efficiency improved 28% on a contribution-margin basis.
  • Program was fully funded from reallocated budget — zero net new spend.

For teams considering a similar program, the lesson is less about any single lever and more about sequencing: pricing first to stabilize margin, retention second to compound the base, and acquisition last so that every incremental visitor lands on a fully tuned funnel.
